    Glossary & References

    Key terms used throughout this guide, followed by primary references, legal sources, and further reading. Terms are defined in the context of Swiss oral history research at UZH.

    Glossary

    Amtsgeheimnis (Official Secrecy)
    The duty of confidentiality that applies to all employees of cantonal institutions in Zurich, including UZH. Governed by the IDG. UZH employees do not need to sign a separate confidentiality declaration — they are bound by official secrecy by virtue of their employment.
    Anonymization
    The irreversible removal of all identifying information from personal data, such that the data can no longer be linked to any individual. Truly anonymized data falls outside the scope of data protection law. Compare with: Pseudonymization.
    Biometric data
    Data resulting from specific technical processing of physical, physiological, or behavioral characteristics that allows or confirms unique identification of a natural person. In oral history, audio recordings (voice) and video recordings (face) constitute biometric data by default.
    Consent (informed)
    A freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous expression of will by which the data subject agrees to the processing of their personal data. For sensitive personal data under Swiss law, consent must be explicit (i.e., cannot be implied from behavior). See Consent Form Templates.
    Controller (Verantwortlicher)
    The person or body that determines the purposes and means of data processing. In a UZH oral history project, the lead researcher (or UZH as an institution) is typically the controller.
    Cross-border transfer
    The transfer of personal data to a country outside Switzerland. Permitted if the destination country provides adequate protection (Federal Council adequacy list), or if appropriate safeguards are in place (Standard Contractual Clauses, explicit consent).
    DaSCH
    Data and Service Center for the Humanities. A Swiss national infrastructure for long-term preservation and access of digital humanities research data. SNSF-supported.
    De-identification key
    A document that maps pseudonyms to real identifiers (e.g., "[NARRATOR-A]" → "Dr. Maria Müller"). Must be stored separately from the anonymized data, at the same or higher security level as the original data.
    DMP (Data Management Plan)
    A document that describes how research data will be collected, processed, stored, shared, and archived. Required by SNSF (via mySNF) and ERC. See Project Planning.
    DPA (Data Processing Agreement)
    A contract between a controller and a processor that specifies data processing conditions, security measures, breach notification, and sub-processing restrictions. Required when using external services (transcription, cloud storage).
    DPIA (Data Protection Impact Assessment / DSFA)
    A systematic analysis of a data processing operation to assess its risks to data subjects and identify mitigation measures. Required for high-risk processing under both the nFADP and GDPR. Recommended for large-scale oral history projects with sensitive data.
    DSG / nFADP
    The Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (Datenschutzgesetz), revised version in force since 1 September 2023. Also referred to as nFADP (new Federal Act on Data Protection) or revDSG. Applies to private persons and federal bodies. See Legal & Ethical Framework.
    Dublin Core
    A set of 15 metadata elements (title, creator, subject, description, date, etc.) widely used for resource description. The SNSF requires Dublin Core as a minimum metadata standard. See Processing & Documentation.
    EDÖB / FDPIC
    The Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner (Eidgenössischer Datenschutz- und Öffentlichkeitsbeauftragter). The Swiss federal supervisory authority for data protection. → edoeb.admin.ch
    Embargo
    A restriction on access to archived materials for a specified period (commonly 10, 25, or 50 years from recording). Used for Tier 4 oral histories. After the embargo expires, access may be opened (often to Tier 3 conditions) if the consent form permits.
    FAIR principles
    Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable. A set of principles for research data management endorsed by SNSF, ERC, and the broader scientific community. "As open as possible, as closed as necessary."
    GTC (General Terms and Conditions)
    Standard contract terms. UZH has specific GTC documents for third-party data processing: GTC CSI (ICT services), GTC DP Outsourcing IT (IT services), GTC DP Processing Third Parties (non-IT services). See Storage & Security.
    HRA / HFG (Human Research Act)
    The Swiss Humanforschungsgesetz. Governs research on human diseases and the structure/function of the human body. Requires approval by a cantonal ethics commission (KEK). Most oral history does not fall under the HRA.
    IDG
    The Zurich Act on Information and Data Protection (Gesetz über die Information und den Datenschutz). The primary data protection law for UZH as a cantonal institution. Governs all data processing by UZH employees.
    KEK
    Cantonal Ethics Commission Zurich (Kantonale Ethikkommission Zürich). Approves research projects falling under the HRA. → zh.ch/ethikkommission
    Narrator
    The person being interviewed in an oral history project. Also referred to as "interviewee," "informant," or "participant" in other disciplines. This guide uses "narrator" following OHA convention, emphasizing their active role in shaping the testimony.
    OHA
    Oral History Association. The professional organization for oral historians, primarily US-based but internationally influential. Its Principles and Best Practices (revised 2018) are a key reference. → oralhistory.org
    Personality rights (Art. 28 ZGB)
    Swiss civil law protections for personal attributes: image, voice, privacy, and honor. Publishing a recording of someone without their consent may violate these rights. Consent from the narrator addresses this for the narrator; third parties remain protected.
    Processor (Auftragsbearbeiter)
    A person or body that processes personal data on behalf of the controller. In oral history: transcription services, cloud hosting providers, external research assistants. Requires a DPA and UZH GTC.
    Pseudonymization
    Replacing identifying information with codes or pseudonyms while maintaining a key that could re-link the data to the individual. Pseudonymized data is still personal data under Swiss law. Compare with: Anonymization.
    Sensitive personal data
    Under Swiss law (nFADP Art. 5(c), IDG): data on religious, philosophical, political, or trade union views/activities; health data; data on ethnicity; genetic data; biometric data; social assistance data; data on administrative or criminal proceedings or sanctions. Processing requires explicit consent.
    Sensitivity tier
    The five-level classification model used in this guide to determine security, access, and consent requirements: Tier 1 (Open), Tier 2 (Conditional), Tier 3 (Restricted), Tier 4 (Embargoed), Tier 5 (Highly Restricted). See Sensitivity Classification Model.
    SWISSUbase
    A national platform for sharing and preserving social science and humanities research data in Switzerland. FAIR-compliant. Supports multiple access levels. SNSF-recommended. → swissubase.ch
    Two-stage consent
    The consent process recommended in this guide: Stage 1 (Information Sheet before the interview) + Stage 2 (Consent Form after the interview). Satisfies both OHA narrator-agency principles and Swiss data protection information obligations.
    VeraCrypt
    Free, open-source encryption software for creating encrypted volumes (containers) and encrypting entire drives. Used in this guide for file-level encryption of oral history data, especially for Tiers 3–5.
    Whisper
    An open-source AI speech recognition model developed by OpenAI. When run locally (not via API), recordings never leave the researcher's device, making it suitable for all sensitivity tiers. See Transcription Tools.
