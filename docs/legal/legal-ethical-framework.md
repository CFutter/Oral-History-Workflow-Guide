# Legal & Ethical Framework

<p class="intro-text">
  This section covers the data protection and ethical framework that applies to oral history research at the University of Zurich. Understanding which laws govern your project is the essential first step.
</p>

<div class="highlight-box">
  <p><strong>Key point:</strong> As a cantonal institution, UZH is primarily governed by the Zurich Act on Information and Data Protection (IDG) — not the federal nFADP. However, the nFADP and potentially the GDPR may also apply depending on your project's scope and collaborations. This section helps you navigate all three layers.</p>
</div>

---

## 2.1 Which Law Applies?

Swiss data protection law is not a single statute. Which law governs your oral history project depends on your institutional affiliation and the nature of your research.

<div class="legal-layer">
  <span class="tag tag-primary">Primary — applies to all UZH projects</span>
  <h3>Zurich Act on Information and Data Protection (IDG)</h3>
  <p>As a cantonal institution, UZH is governed by the Zurich <em>Gesetz über die Information und den Datenschutz</em> (IDG) and its implementing ordinance (IDV). All UZH employees are bound by official secrecy (<em>Amtsgeheimnis</em>) under the IDG without needing to sign an additional confidentiality declaration.</p>
  <p>Key provisions for oral history:</p>
  <ul>
    <li><strong>§ 6 IDG — Third-party processing:</strong> External services (transcription, cloud hosting) require specific legal, technical, and organizational measures. UZH has standard documents for this (see Section 2.8).</li>
    <li><strong>§ 7 IDG — Security measures:</strong> Appropriate technical and organizational measures are required to protect information.</li>
    <li><strong>§ 8–9 IDG — Proportionality and purpose limitation:</strong> Data may only be processed to the extent necessary and for the stated purpose.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>Supervisory authority: Cantonal Data Protection Commissioner (<em>Datenschutzbeauftragte des Kantons Zürich</em>) → <a href="https://www.datenschutz.ch">datenschutz.ch</a></p>
</div>

<div class="legal-layer">
  <span class="tag tag-secondary">Reference standard — always relevant</span>
  <h3>Federal Act on Data Protection (nFADP / revDSG)</h3>
  <p>The revised <em>Bundesgesetz über den Datenschutz</em> (DSG), entered into force on 1 September 2023. It applies to <strong>private persons</strong> and <strong>federal bodies</strong> (including ETH Zurich, but <em>not</em> cantonal universities like UZH).</p>
  <p>UZH researchers must still understand it because:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>It applies when collaborating with <strong>private-sector partners</strong> or <strong>federal institutions</strong></li>
    <li>It defines the <strong>national baseline</strong> for concepts like sensitive data, consent, and cross-border transfer</li>
    <li><strong>Grant funders</strong> (SNSF, ERC) may reference federal or EU-level standards</li>
    <li>It carries <strong>personal criminal liability</strong> of up to CHF 250,000 for intentional violations</li>
  </ul>
  <p>Supervisory authority: Federal Data Protection and Information Commissioner (FDPIC/EDÖB) → <a href="https://www.edoeb.admin.ch">edoeb.admin.ch</a></p>
</div>

<div class="legal-layer">
  <span class="tag tag-conditional">Conditional — only if health-related</span>
  <h3>Human Research Act (HRA / HFG)</h3>
  <p>The <em>Humanforschungsgesetz</em> governs research on human diseases and the structure/function of the human body. Most oral history projects <strong>do not fall under the HRA</strong>. However, it <em>may</em> apply if:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Interviews focus on <strong>health experiences</strong> (patient oral histories, healthcare workers on clinical practice)</li>
    <li>The research involves <strong>biological material</strong> or <strong>health data</strong> collected specifically for the project</li>
  </ul>
  <p>If the HRA applies → mandatory approval by the <strong>Cantonal Ethics Commission Zurich (KEK)</strong> before data collection.</p>
  <p>→ <a href="https://www.zh.ch/de/gesundheitsdirektion/ethikkommission.html">KEK Zurich</a></p>
</div>

!!! tip "Quick decision"
    Is your research about human diseases or the structure/function of the human body? **Yes** → contact KEK. **No** → the HRA does not apply, but faculty-level ethical review and data protection obligations remain.

## 2.2 nFADP vs. GDPR

If your project involves narrators in the EU, cross-border collaboration, or EU-funded grants, you may also need to comply with the GDPR. Key differences:

| Aspect | nFADP (Switzerland) | GDPR (EU) |
|---|---|---|
| **Sensitive data categories** | Includes genetic + biometric (since 2023) | Same categories |
| **Consent for sensitive data** | Explicit consent required | Explicit consent required |
| **DPO requirement** | Not mandatory | Mandatory in certain cases |
| **Breach notification** | "As soon as possible" to FDPIC | Within 72 hours |
| **Fines** | Up to CHF 250,000 on **individuals** | Up to €20M / 4% turnover on **organizations** |
| **Research exemption** | Art. 31(2)(e): narrow | Art. 89: broader |
| **DPIA** | Required for high-risk processing | Required for high-risk processing |

!!! note "Practical takeaway"
    If you already comply with the GDPR (e.g., for an ERC project), you will meet most nFADP requirements. The main additional Swiss concern is **personal criminal liability** for intentional violations.

## 2.3 Sensitive Personal Data in Oral History

Understanding what counts as "sensitive" under Swiss law determines consent requirements, storage security, and access restrictions.

Under both the nFADP (Art. 5(c)) and the Zurich IDG, the following are **particularly sensitive** (*besonders schützenswerte Personendaten*):

| Category | Common in oral history? | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Religious or philosophical beliefs | Yes | Faith journey, conversion, spiritual practices |
| Political opinions or activities | Yes | Political activism, party membership, voting |
| Health data | Yes | Illness, disability, mental health, medical treatment |
| Data on ethnicity or race | Yes | Ethnic identity, migration, discrimination |
| Trade union activities | Sometimes | Labor organizing, workplace disputes |
| Social assistance | Sometimes | Welfare, disability benefits |
| Administrative or criminal proceedings | Sometimes | Arrest, trial, legal dispute |
| Genetic data | Rare | Hereditary conditions, genetic testing |
| Biometric data | **Yes — by default** | Audio of a voice, video of a face |

!!! warning "Audio and video recordings"
    An audio or video recording of an identifiable person is, in itself, personal data. When the narrator discusses **any** sensitive category above, you are processing **sensitive personal data**. This is the default in most oral history projects. Plan accordingly from the start.

**Third-party data:** Narrators frequently mention other people by name. When a narrator describes a colleague's political views or a family member's health condition, you are processing sensitive personal data about **third parties who have not consented**. See Section 2.7 for strategies.

## 2.4 Informed Consent

Informed consent is the cornerstone of ethical oral history practice and a legal requirement under Swiss law.

### What consent must cover

Valid consent must be **informed** (narrator understands what, why, how, who, where, how long), **voluntary** (no pressure or coercion), **specific** (covers stated purposes), **documented** (written — UZH standard), and **explicit** for sensitive data (implied consent is insufficient).

### The timing tension

There is a tension between oral history practice and data protection law:

- **OHA best practice** recommends signing the legal release **after** the interview, so the narrator can decide about sensitive topics that emerged.
- **Swiss data protection law** requires information **before or at the point of** data collection.

### Recommended: Two-stage consent

=== "Stage 1 — Before the interview"
    Provide the narrator with a written **information sheet** describing:

    - The project, its objectives, and the lead researcher
    - Types of data collected (audio, video, transcript)
    - Processing purposes and methods
    - Storage location and security measures
    - Who will have access
    - Retention period (default: 10 years per UZH standard)
    - Narrator's rights (access, correction, deletion, withdrawal)

    Obtain **preliminary consent** to record the interview.

=== "Stage 2 — After the interview"
    Review the consent form together with the narrator. The narrator now makes informed decisions about:

    - Access restrictions and sensitivity tier
    - Pseudonymization or name attribution
    - Embargo periods
    - Exclusion of specific passages
    - Conditions for third-party anonymization
    - Consent to secondary use / future research

    Obtain the **final, detailed consent** (the legal release).

This two-stage process satisfies both the OHA principle of narrator agency and the legal requirement for prior information.

### Withdrawal

Under Swiss law, a narrator may withdraw consent at any time for future processing. Per the UZH consent form: data collected up to that point must be deleted and destroyed, unless doing so would make it impossible to achieve the research objectives or seriously jeopardize their achievement. The consent form should clearly explain what happens upon withdrawal.

### Secondary use

If archived oral histories may be reused, the original consent must address this. Options:

- **Project-specific:** Limited to current project only — reuse requires re-consent
- **Broad consent:** Future academic research within a defined scope
- **Tiered consent** (recommended): The narrator chooses from options

## 2.5 Ethical Review at UZH

### When is ethical review required?

**If the HRA applies** → Mandatory approval by KEK before data collection.

**If the HRA does not apply** (most oral history) → No legal requirement, but UZH's Ethics Policy recommends review for projects involving:

- Vulnerable groups
- Sensitive data categories
- Potential psychological or social harm
- Deception or incomplete disclosure
- Dependency relationships

### Self-assessment checklist

- [ ] Does your project involve **vulnerable groups** (minors, persons with mental illness/dementia, persons in prison, persons incapable of judgment)?
- [ ] Will you collect **sensitive data** (religion, politics, health, ethnicity, criminal proceedings, sexual orientation)?
- [ ] Could participation **psychologically harm** the narrator (re-traumatization, emotional distress)?
- [ ] Could participation **socially harm** the narrator (reputational damage)?
- [ ] Is there a **dependency relationship** between you and the narrator?
- [ ] Does your **funder or publisher** require evidence of ethical review?

If you answered "yes" to any → contact your faculty's ethics committee.

!!! info "UZH Ethics Commission"
    The university-wide UZH Ethics Commission (Center for Ethics) supports ethical awareness but is **not responsible for evaluating individual research projects**. For project-level review, contact your faculty's ethics committee. → [ethik.uzh.ch](https://www.ethik.uzh.ch/en/ethikkommission.html)

## 2.6 Personality Rights and Copyright

### Personality rights (Art. 28 Swiss Civil Code)

Every person has the right to protection against unlawful interference with their personality:

- **Right to one's own image and voice** — publishing a recording without consent violates this
- **Right to privacy** — disclosing private facts without consent is unlawful
- **Right to honor** — content damaging someone's reputation may be unlawful

Consent from the narrator addresses these for the narrator's personality. **Third parties** remain protected.

### Copyright in interviews

Copyright is shared: the **narrator** holds copyright in their spoken words (if meeting the originality threshold), the **interviewer** may hold copyright in the questions and structure, and the **recording** is a neighboring right held by the producer. The consent/release form should include a transfer or license of rights permitting the intended use.

## 2.7 Third-Party Rights

When a narrator says *"My supervisor was a terrible bully who had a drinking problem,"* you are recording identifiable personal data about someone who has not consented.

### Practical strategies by tier

=== "Tier 1–2 (Open)"
    - Anonymize all third parties by default unless they are public figures in their public capacity
    - Use descriptions: "a colleague" instead of "Dr. Müller"
    - Obtain consent from named third parties if their identifiable data is central and will be published

=== "Tier 3 (Restricted)"
    - Anonymize third parties in any publicly accessible output
    - Original recording with identifiable third-party data retained under restricted access
    - Note in metadata which passages contain third-party data

=== "Tier 4–5 (Closed)"
    - Third-party data may be retained in the original recording
    - Access restrictions protect third-party interests during embargo
    - Plan for anonymization or review when access opens

## 2.8 Third-Party Data Processing at UZH {: #third-party-data }

When you engage external service providers (transcription, cloud storage, hosting), UZH's standardized framework applies:

**For ICT services** (cloud transcription, hosted platforms):

1. GTC of the Swiss Conference on Informatics (CSI) for ICT Services (Jan 2015)
2. GTC DP Outsourcing IT UZH (July 2018)
3. UZH Confidentiality Declaration (signed by all persons with access to UZH data)

**For non-ICT services** (human transcribers, research assistants):

1. GTC DP Processing Third Parties UZH (July 2018)
2. UZH Confidentiality Declaration

**For processing in countries without adequate data protection:**

- Add: EU Standard Contractual Clauses (C(2010)593) adapted for UZH

!!! danger "Consumer AI tools"
    Free consumer-grade transcription tools (free Otter.ai, YouTube auto-caption, consumer Google Docs voice typing) typically do not offer data processing agreements and may use your data for model training. **Do not use these for oral history recordings at any sensitivity tier.**

## 2.9 Data Sharing and Cross-Border Transfer

**Within UZH:** Generally permissible if recipients need the data for a stated purpose, access is limited by tier, and sharing is consistent with the narrator's consent.

**With external partners:** Requires a **Data Transfer and Use Agreement (DTUA)** or the UZH **Master Data Sharing Agreement** (Controller–Controller).

**Cross-border transfer:** Verify that the destination country provides adequate protection (Federal Council list). If not → use EU/Swiss Standard Contractual Clauses or obtain explicit consent for the transfer.

---

## 2.10 Vulnerable Populations

Groups requiring additional protections:

- Persons with mental illness or dementia
- Persons in prison or acute emergencies
- Minors (under 18)
- Persons incapable of judgment
- Communities with historically complex relationships with researchers
- Persons in dependency relationships with the researcher
- Persons whose testimony could endanger them (undocumented migrants, whistleblowers)

**Key considerations:** Ensure capacity to consent. Consider whether participation could cause harm. Offer pseudonyms from the outset. For specific communities, consider a community advisory board. Classify at **Tier 3 or higher**. Plan for interviewer wellbeing when working with traumatic testimony.

---

## 2.11 Record-Keeping Obligations

**Processing activities register:** Document your oral history project with purpose, data categories, recipients, retention periods, and security measures.

**Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA):** Required when processing poses high risk. Likely needed for large-scale sensitive oral history projects. Even when not legally required, a DPIA is good practice.

## 2.12 Legal Checklist

- [ ] Identified applicable law (IDG primary + nFADP + HRA if health-related)
- [ ] Assessed sensitivity tier
- [ ] Prepared informed consent forms (adapted from UZH template, tier-specific)
- [ ] Determined whether ethical review is needed
- [ ] Completed or planned a Data Management Plan
- [ ] Assessed third-party processing needs and prepared UZH documents
- [ ] Planned anonymization/pseudonymization strategy for third-party data
- [ ] Verified cross-border transfer compliance if data leaves Switzerland
- [ ] Considered a DPIA if processing sensitive data at scale
- [ ] Registered processing activities
- [ ] Consulted UZH Delegate for Data Protection for project-specific questions
